HAFT KHAN is a third-person action-puzzle game based on the Seven Trials of Rostam — the greatest hero in Persian mythology — drawn from Ferdowsi's Shahnameh, the 50,000-verse national epic of Iran, written around the year 1000 AD. It is to Persian civilization what Homer's Odyssey is to Greece. It has never been adapted into a premium game. HAFT KHAN intends to change that.

02 — The Game

Show Me the Game

"Your horse is smarter than most people. He is also your entire HUD."

You play as Rostam — enormous, near-immortal, carrying a mace the size of a tree. Your horse Rakhsh has saved your life more times than you can count. In HAFT KHAN, Rakhsh is not just a mount — he is your compass, your threat detector, your stamina gauge, and your conscience. His breathing, his ears, his refusal to move forward: these are your UI. There is no minimap. There is no waypoint marker. There is just a very wise horse and seven mythic worlds trying to destroy you both.

The Seven Worlds

I
The Golden Steppe

Tutorial district. Open grasslands. The world teaches you everything through Rakhsh before it becomes dangerous.

II
River Reed Forest

Dense waterlogged jungle. Visibility collapses. Sound becomes survival.

III
Ruins of Dej-e Maran

A serpent city frozen mid-collapse. Puzzle-heavy. Architecture as enemy.

IV
The Darkwood

No light source permitted. Rakhsh is blind. You navigate by sound and memory.

V
Lion's Domain

A savannah ruled by a divine beast. Combat becomes ritual. Brute force will fail you.

VI–VII
The Witch's Court & The Dragon's Throne

Deception, sorcery, and the game's final reckoning. Every mechanic converges.

Four Design Principles

Invisible Onboarding

No tutorial prompts. The world teaches through consequence.

Progressive Diegetic HUD

Every UI element is inside the world. Rakhsh is the interface.

Alternating Rhythm

Combat and puzzle beats trade off. Neither overstays its welcome.

No Minimap. Ever.

Spatial awareness is a skill to develop, not a crutch to rely on.

03 — Market

Why Should This Game Exist Now?

The games market is saturated with Norse mythology, Greek mythology, and Western medieval fantasy. Players are hungry for worlds they have not already memorized. HAFT KHAN offers something genuinely new to a global audience — and something deeply resonant to the 300+ million people across Iran, Afghanistan, Tajikistan, and the broader Persian-speaking world for whom the Shahnameh is cultural bedrock.

Comparable Titles

Title What We Borrow What We Do Differently
Shadow of the Colossus Sparse world, diegetic design, mythic weight Multi-world structure, mounted combat with full agency
God of War (2018) Third-person action, narrative epic, cultural depth Persian IP, puzzle emphasis, no omnipresent narrator
Assassin's Creed (Origins/Odyssey) Historical/mythological world-building, combat Concentrated 7-world structure, no open world bloat
Prince of Persia: Sands of Time Persian aesthetic, acrobatic puzzle-action Authentic epic source, not Western interpretation of Persia

Target Audience

  • Primary: 22–38 year old PC/console players who completed God of War and Shadow of the Colossus and actively seek narrative-driven, world-rich action games.
  • Secondary: Persian, Iranian, Afghan, and Central Asian diaspora players (est. 10M+ globally) who have never seen their mythology represented in a premium game.
  • Tertiary: History, mythology, and world literature enthusiasts who engage with games as cultural artifacts.

The Gap

No premium action game has ever been built authentically on the Shahnameh. Not one. The IP is 1,000 years old, universally recognized across the Middle East and Central Asia, completely public domain, and completely untouched by the games industry. That is the gap. HAFT KHAN fills it.
